## **Draft Literature Review**

### **1. Foundations of Design Thinking**

Design Thinking (DT) is a human-centered, iterative approach to problem-solving that emphasizes understanding user needs, ideation, prototyping, and testing. As described by Brown (2009), DT provides a structured yet flexible framework that encourages creativity and innovation across diverse domains. Plattner, Meinel, and Leifer (2011) further elaborate on the academic foundation of DT, outlining methods and tools suitable for embedding participatory practices. Liedtka and Ogilvie (2011) complement these perspectives with a practical toolkit approach, showing how ideation and prototyping stages can benefit from structured exercises that engage stakeholders. While these works provide a solid understanding of the DT process, they largely focus on conceptual frameworks and offer limited guidance on specific participatory tools like Design Games.

---

### **2. Design Games and Co-Creation in Design Thinking**

Design Games are participatory tools used to engage users and stakeholders in co-creation, facilitating exploration of ideas, uncovering user needs, and aligning diverse perspectives (Sanders & Stappers, 2008). Brandt, Messeter, and Binder (2008) classify such tools into generative, evaluative, and communicative categories, highlighting how they can enrich ideation and prototyping activities. Bjögvinsson, Ehn, and Hillgren (2012) examine the integration of participatory design methods in complex systems, showing that Design Games can bridge gaps between abstract ideas and tangible prototypes. Specifically, Brandt (2006) provides a framework for exploratory Design Games, detailing how these tools can support multiple DT steps, from empathizing with users to developing initial prototypes. These studies demonstrate the potential of Design Games to complement DT processes, especially in early-stage ideation and prototyping.

---

### **3. Embedding Design Games in Prototyping and Ideation**

Several studies illustrate practical applications of Design Games in prototyping. Visser et al. (2005) describe “contextmapping,” where games help elicit user needs and preferences, providing actionable insights for prototype development. Halskov and Dalsgaard (2006) present card-based workshops as Design Games for structured ideation, showing that these tools encourage creativity while maintaining a user-centered focus. Björklund and Holmström (2016) extend these ideas to serious games, highlighting their effectiveness in engaging users during co-design sessions. Across these studies, the integration of Design Games in prototyping and ideation has been shown to foster collaboration, generate innovative concepts, and translate abstract ideas into tangible artifacts.

---

### **4. Challenges and Limitations of Design Games**

Despite their benefits, Design Games face several limitations. Binder, Brandt, and Gregory (2008) note that adoption in real-world projects can be inconsistent, with issues related to facilitation, documentation, and resource requirements. Ehn (2008) further observes that many games are not self-documenting, making it difficult to capture insights for later stages of DT. Müller and Druin (2012) and Müller and Schulz (2018) highlight additional challenges, including measuring outcomes, scaling games for larger groups, and integrating digital tools effectively. These studies point to gaps in existing methodology, suggesting a need for structured documentation, method extensions, and strategies to increase adoption and usability.

---

### **5. Research Gaps**

While prior literature establishes that Design Games can enhance Design Thinking by engaging users and supporting ideation and prototyping, several gaps remain. There is limited research on systematic embedding of Design Games across all DT steps, particularly in complex system design. Documentation challenges and underutilization of Design Games indicate the need for method extensions that make games more self-documenting and easier to integrate. Furthermore, existing studies often focus on isolated case studies; there is an opportunity to synthesize findings to provide a higher-level framework for integrating Design Games in user-centered system design processes.

## 1. Kritik und Forschungslücken zu Design Games

In aktuellen Veröffentlichungen (u. a. im [ACM Digital Library](https://dl.acm.org/doi/fullHtml/10.1145/3604571.3604574) und [MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/2075-4698/11/4/137) diskutiert) werden folgende Defizite benannt:

- Mangelnde Dokumentation: Dies ist eines der größten Hindernisse. Games werden oft als flüchtig wahrgenommen; Ergebnisse entstehen im Moment ("in the wild"), werden aber selten systematisch in Artefakte überführt, die für die spätere Systementwicklung nutzbar sind.
- Fehlende Wirkungsanalyse (Impact Assessment): Es gibt kaum empirische Langzeitstudien, die belegen, dass Design Games tatsächlich zu "besseren" Produkten führen. Die Forschung konzentriert sich meist auf den Prozess und den unmittelbaren "Spaßfaktor", vernachlässigt aber messbare Outcomes.
- Methodische Strenge vs. Unterhaltung: Kritiker wie [Johansson-Sköldberg et al.](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/335725966_The_Shortcomings_of_Design_Thinking_when_Compared_to_Designerly_Thinking) (2013) bemängeln, dass Design-Thinking-Methoden (einschließlich Games) oft als "Manager-Trend" ohne wissenschaftliche Tiefe angewandt werden. In der Praxis verkommen sie dann zu bloßen Icebreakern.
- Machtasymmetrien: Neuere Arbeiten (z. B. [Brandt & Binder](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/268779683_Taking_Design_Games_Seriously_-_Re-connecting_Situated_Power_Relations_of_People_and_Materials) 2014) reflektieren selbstkritisch, dass die Materialien und Regeln der Spiele oft unbewusst Machtverhältnisse zementieren oder bestimmte Stakeholder ausschließen können.
